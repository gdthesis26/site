RESIST: Graphic Design Senior Show

VIVAN NADKARNI

ENCRYPTION as resistance

Installation Instructions

Step inside the fabric booth and allow the curtain to close fully behind you. This space is designed to offer a brief moment of privacy away from the gallery floor. Inside, you will be presented with a question about the current administration, surveillance, discrimination, and your personal complicity in these matters.

Once you submit your response, your words are immediately transformed into an abstract geometric cipher and printed using a thermal printer. Your original text is never displayed publicly. You may take one print with you and are invited to place the duplicate print onto the wall outside the booth. Over time, these collected ciphers form a growing visual archive of a community expressing dissent while being protected.

Please interact gently with the booth, printer, and posted prints. Visitors are welcome to spend time observing the wall of accumulated ciphers, but we ask that no existing prints be photographed at close range in ways that attempt to decode another participant’s response.

Extended Project Description

What does it mean for people to speak out when what they say can carry real consequences? As an international student in the United States, an invisible pressure to self-censor any speech that could carry political meaning weighs down on you at all times, especially in a highly surveilled environment, where saying the wrong thing can even lead to deportation.

Living within an extensive surveillance net is relatively new for the global north, but for historically oppressed people, this is not new at all. A lot of these communities were forced to develop covert forms of communication to circumvent the surveillance nets they are subjected to. One such practice, Tatreez, is a Palestinian embroidery practice of encoding messages into decorative patterns on fabric. To most, it seems only ornamental, but to their community, each stitch carries important information.

Encryption as Resistance spawned from the idea of reframing encryption as a tool to create visible resistance that still protects those who speak up. The project takes the form of an interactive installation that invites participants to enter a booth and respond to a question about contemporary political realities such as governance, global conflict, and foreign policy. Their responses are never stored or displayed as text, and are instead encoded into an abstract geometric pattern and printed as a visual cipher.

Individually, the artifacts produced by the installation act as private expressions of political dissent that allow those who cannot speak up an opportunity to do so while protecting the meaning held within the pattern. Collectively, when displayed together, they form a field of encoded voices that assert a shared voice through the similarities in the patterns formed, creating a symbolic community.

Living within the tension between the need for visibility and privacy, Encryption as Resistance proposes an alternative mode of resistance: one in which dissent can be seen and shared without fear of being exposed.

Biography

Vivan Nadkarni is a multidisciplinary designer and artist whose work spans identity, publication, campaign, exhibition, and interaction design. His practice is grounded in deep research and the development of flexible visual systems that translate complex concepts into clear, engaging forms. He is interested in how design can influence understanding and communication across differences, as well as decolonial frameworks outside of the Western design canon.

Raised in the vibrant, bustling, and culturally layered city of Mumbai, Vivan grew up surrounded by thousands of overlapping and conflicting languages, social realities, and cultures. This environment informs the curiosity, adaptability, and empathy with which he approaches design problems, as well as his sensitivity to how communication operates across varied audiences and contexts.

He is currently a senior in the Graphic Design BFA program at California College of the Arts in San Francisco, where he also serves as Program Chair Assistant.

Alongside his design practice, Vivan enjoys reading (mostly fantasy and magical realism) and spends his free time drawing short comics based on events from his everyday life in his sketchbook. He loves watching movies (and logging them on Letterboxd right after) and listening to music, especially electronic and cloud rap.
